'''Alexander''' ({{IPAc-en|ˈ|æ|l|ə|g|z|ˈ|æ|n|d|ər}}, {{IPAc-en|ˈ|æ|l|ə|g|z|ˈ|ɑː|n|d|ər}}) is a common male first name, and less common [[Alexander (surname)|surname]] derived from the [[Greek language|Greek]] &quot;Αλέξανδρος&quot; (Aléxandros). The most famous is [[Alexander the Great]], who created one of the largest empires in ancient history.

==Origin==
[[Etymology|Etymologically]], the name is derived from the [[Greek language|Greek]] {{lang|grc-gre|Ἀλέξανδρος}} (Aléxandros), meaning &quot;Defender of the people&quot; or &quot;Defending men&quot;&lt;ref&gt;{{LSJ|a)le/candros|Ἀλέξανδρος|ref}}.&lt;/ref&gt; and also, &quot;Protector of men&quot;, a compound of the verb {{lang|grc|ἀλέξειν}} ''alexein'', &quot;to ward off, to avert, to defend&quot;&lt;ref&gt;{{LSJ|a)le/cw|ἀλέξειν|shortref}}.&lt;/ref&gt; and the noun {{lang|grc|ἀνήρ}} ''anēr'', &quot;man&quot; (&lt;small&gt;[[Genitive|GEN]]&lt;/small&gt; {{lang|grc|ἀνδρός}} ''andros'').&lt;ref&gt;{{LSJ|a)nh/r|ἀνήρ|shortref}}.&lt;/ref&gt; It is an example of the widespread motif of Greek (or Indo-European more generally) names expressing &quot;battle-prowess&quot;, in this case the ability to withstand or push back an enemy [[shield wall|battle line]].{{citation needed|date=November 2012}}

The earliest attested form of the name is the [[Mycenaean Greek]] feminine [[anthroponym]] {{lang|gmy|𐀀𐀩𐀏𐀭𐀅𐀨}}, ''a-re-ka-sa-da-ra'', (transcribed as ''[[Alexandra]]''), written in the [[Linear B]] syllabic script.&lt;ref&gt;Tablet [[Mycenae|MY]] V 659 (61). {{cite web|url=http://www.palaeolexicon.com/default.aspx?static=12&amp;wid=217|title=The Linear B word a-re-ka-sa-da-ra|website=Palaeolexicon. Word study tool of ancient languages}} {{cite web|url=https://www2.hf.uio.no/damos/Index/item/chosen_item_id/5622|title=MY 659 V (61)|website=DĀMOS Database of Mycenaean at Oslo|publisher=[[University of Oslo]]}} {{cite web|last=Raymoure|first=K.A.|url=http://minoan.deaditerranean.com/resources/linear-b-sign-groups/a/a-re-ka-sa-da-ra-qe/|title=a-re-ka-sa-da-ra-qe| work=Deaditerranean. Minoan Linear A &amp; Mycenaean Linear B}}&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref&gt;{{cite book|title=The Mycenaean World|first=John|last=Chadwick|author1-link=John Chadwick|place=New York|publisher=Cambridge University Press|origyear=1976|year=1999}}&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref&gt;[http://web.archive.org/web/20080627021042/http://www.geocities.com/kurogr/linearb.pdf Mycenaean (Linear B)&amp;nbsp;– English Glossary]&lt;/ref&gt;

The name was one of the titles (&quot;epithets&quot;) given to the Greek goddess [[Hera]] and as such is usually taken to mean &quot;one who comes to save warriors&quot;. In the [[Iliad]], the character [[Paris (mythology)|Paris]] is known also as Alexander.&lt;ref&gt;[http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus%3Atext%3A1999.04.0073%3Aentry%3D*%29ale%2Fcandros Ἀλέξανδρος],
Georg Autenrieth, ''A Homeric Dictionary'', on Perseus Digital Library&lt;/ref&gt; The name's popularity was spread throughout the Greek world by the military conquests of [[Alexander the Great|King Alexander III, commonly known as &quot;Alexander the Great&quot;]]. Most later Alexanders in various countries were directly or indirectly named for him.{{citation needed|date=June 2010}}
