'''Robert (Bob) M. Frankston''' (born June 14, 1949 in [[Brooklyn]], New York) is the co-creator with [[Dan Bricklin]] of the [[VisiCalc]] spreadsheet program and the co-founder of [[Software Arts]], the company that developed it.

Frankston graduated in 1966 from [[Stuyvesant High School]] in [[New York City]]&lt;ref&gt;{{cite web |url=http://www.frankston.com/public/Bob_Frankston_Bio.asp |title=Bob Frankston - bio |accessdate=2007-10-31}}&lt;/ref&gt; and in 1970 from [[Massachusetts Institute of Technology|M.I.T.]]

Frankston has received numerous honors and awards for his work:

* [[Fellow]] of the [[Association for Computing Machinery]] (1994) &quot;for the invention of VisiCalc, a new metaphor for data manipulation that galvanized the personal computing industry&quot;
* MIT William L. Stewart Award for co-founding the M.I.T. [[SIPB|Student Information Processing Board]].
* The Association for Computing Machinery Software System Award (1985)
* The [[CSAIL|MIT LCS]] Industrial Achievement Award
* The Washington Award (2001) from the [[Western Society of Engineers]] (with Bricklin)
* In 2004, he was made a Fellow of the [[Computer History Museum]] &quot;for advancing the utility of personal computers by developing the VisiCalc electronic spreadsheet.&quot;&lt;ref&gt; {{cite web
 | url = http://www.computerhistory.org/fellowawards/hall/bios/Bob,Frankston/
 | title = Bob Frankston
 | publisher = Computer History Museum
 | accessdate = 2013-05-23
}} &lt;/ref&gt;

In recent years, Frankston has been an outspoken advocate for reducing the role of telecommunications companies in the evolution of the internet, particularly with respect to broadband and mobile communications.&lt;ref&gt;Bob Frankston. The 2 Trillion Dollar Mistake. Presentation at Emerging Communications Conference 2008.&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref&gt;{{cite web |url=http://frankston.com/public/?name=IntroAmbient |title=Ambient Connectivity |accessdate=2009-12-30}}&lt;/ref&gt; He coined the term &quot;Regulatorium&quot; to describe what he considers collusion between telecommunication companies and their regulators that prevents change.&lt;ref&gt;{{cite web |url=http://www.frankston.com/public/?name=1076980277296-19607 |title=The Regulatorium and the Moral Imperative |accessdate=2009-12-30}}&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref&gt;{{cite web |url=http://blogs.law.harvard.edu/doc/2009/11/25/the-infrastructure-dynamic/ |author=Doc Searls |title=The Infrastructure Dynamic. |accessdate=2009-12-30}}&lt;/ref&gt;
