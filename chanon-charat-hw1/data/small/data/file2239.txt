The '''Major League Baseball Most Valuable Player Award''' ('''MVP''') is an annual [[Major League Baseball]] (MLB) award, given to one outstanding player in the [[American League]] and one in the [[National League]]. Since 1931, it has been awarded by the [[Baseball Writers' Association of America]] (BBWAA). The winners receive the [[Kenesaw Mountain Landis]] Memorial Baseball Award, which became the official name of the award in 1944,&lt;ref name=LandisNBHoF&gt;{{cite web|title=Landis, Kenesaw|publisher=National Baseball Hall of Fame and Museum|url=http://baseballhall.org/hof/landis-kenesaw|accessdate=2011-11-22}}&lt;/ref&gt; in honor of the first [[Commissioner of Baseball (MLB)|MLB commissioner]], who served from 1920 until his death on November 25, 1944.&lt;ref name=LandisNBHoF/&gt;&lt;ref name=&quot;ESPN 1763&quot;&gt;{{cite book|last=Gillette|first=Gary|author2=Palmer, Pete|title=The ESPN Baseball Encyclopedia|publisher=Sterling Publishing Co|location=New York City, New York|year=2007|edition=Fourth|page=1763|isbn=978-1-4027-4771-7}}&lt;/ref&gt;

MVP voting takes place before the [[Major League Baseball postseason|postseason]], but the results are not announced until after the [[World Series]]. The BBWAA began by polling three writers in each league city in 1938, reducing that number to two per league city in 1961.&lt;ref name=&quot;ESPN&quot;&gt;Gillette &amp; Palmer, pp. 1764–1765&lt;/ref&gt; The BBWAA does not offer a clear-cut definition of what &quot;most valuable&quot; means, instead leaving the judgment to the individual voters.&lt;ref&gt;{{cite news |last=Kepner |first=Tyler |title=Where Do You Find Value? Discussing the M.V.P. Criteria |date=September 4, 2011 |newspaper=[[The New York Times]] |page=SP3 |url=http://www.nytimes.com/2011/09/04/sports/baseball/blue-jays-bautista-and-yankees-granderson-discuss-mvp-criteria.html?scp=1&amp;sq=baseball%20mvp&amp;st=cse |accessdate=September 6, 2011}}&lt;/ref&gt;

[[First basemen]], with 34&amp;nbsp;winners, have won the most MVPs among [[infielder]]s, followed by [[second basemen]]&amp;nbsp;(16), [[third basemen]]&amp;nbsp;(15), and [[shortstop]]s&amp;nbsp;(15). Of the 24&amp;nbsp;[[pitcher]]s who have won the award, 15 are [[right-handed]] while 9 are [[left-handed]]. [[Walter Johnson]], [[Carl Hubbell]], and [[Hal Newhouser]] are the only pitchers who have won multiple times, Newhouser winning consecutively in 1944 and 1945.&lt;ref name=&quot;BD 2000&quot;&gt;{{cite journal|date=December 2000|title=Major League Baseball's Most Valuable Player Award Winners|journal=Baseball Digest|publisher=Century Publishing Co.|location=Evanston, Illinois|volume=59|issue=12|pages=86–89|issn=0005-609X|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=my0DAAAAMBAJ&amp;pg=PA86#v=onepage&amp;q=&amp;f=false|accessdate=December 29, 2009}}&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref&gt;http://www.baseball-reference.com/players/j/johnswa01.shtml&lt;/ref&gt;

[[Hank Greenberg]], [[Stan Musial]], [[Alex Rodriguez]], and [[Robin Yount]] have won at different positions,&lt;ref name=&quot;BD 2000&quot; /&gt; while Rodriguez is the only player who has won the award with two different teams at two different positions.&lt;ref name=&quot;Rodriguez&quot;&gt;{{cite web|url=http://www.baseball-reference.com/players/r/rodrial01.shtml|title=Alex Rodriguez Statistics|work=[[Baseball-Reference]]|publisher=Sports Reference LLC|accessdate=December 3, 2009}}&lt;/ref&gt; [[Barry Bonds]] has won the most often&amp;nbsp;(seven times) and the most consecutively&amp;nbsp;(2001–04).&lt;ref name=&quot;Bonds&quot;&gt;{{cite web|url=http://www.baseball-reference.com/players/b/bondsba01.shtml|title=Barry Bonds Statistics|work=[[Baseball-Reference]]|publisher=Sports Reference LLC|accessdate=December 3, 2009}}&lt;/ref&gt; [[Jimmie Foxx]] was the first player to win multiple times;&lt;ref name=&quot;Bonds&quot;&gt;{{cite web|url=http://www.baseball-reference.com/players/f/foxxji01.shtml|title=Jimmie Foxx Statistics|work=[[Baseball-Reference]]|publisher=Sports Reference LLC|accessdate=December 3, 2009}}&lt;/ref&gt; 9&amp;nbsp;players have won three times, and 19 have won twice.&lt;ref name=&quot;winner list&quot;&gt;{{cite web|url=http://www.baseball-reference.com/awards/mvp_cya.shtml|title=Most Valuable Player MVP Awards &amp; Cy Young Awards Winners|work=[[Baseball-Reference]]|publisher=Sports Reference LLC|accessdate=December 3, 2009}}&lt;/ref&gt; Frank Robinson is the only player to win the award in both the American and National Leagues.

The award's only tie occurred in the [[National League]] in 1979, when [[Keith Hernandez]] and [[Willie Stargell]] received an equal number of points.&lt;ref name=&quot;BD 2000&quot; /&gt;&lt;ref name=&quot;1979 awards&quot;&gt;{{cite web|url=http://www.baseball-reference.com/awards/awards_1979.shtml|title=Baseball Awards Voting for 1979|work=[[Baseball-Reference]]|publisher=Sports Reference LLC|accessdate=December 28, 2009}}&lt;/ref&gt; There have been 17 unanimous winners, who received all the first-place votes.&lt;ref name=&quot;ESPN&quot; /&gt; The [[New York Yankees]] have the most winning players with 22, followed by the [[St. Louis Cardinals]] with 17&amp;nbsp;winners. The award has never been presented to a member of the following five teams: [[Arizona Diamondbacks]], [[Miami Marlins]], [[New York Mets]], [[Tampa Bay Rays]], and [[Washington Nationals]]. The most recent recipients are [[Mike Trout]] in the [[American League]] and [[Clayton Kershaw]] in the [[National League]].

In recent decades, pitchers have rarely won the award. When [[Justin Verlander]] won the AL award in 2011, he became the first pitcher in either league to be named the MVP since [[Dennis Eckersley]] in 1992. Verlander also became the first [[starting pitcher]] to win this award since Roger Clemens had accomplished the feat in 1986.&lt;ref&gt;{{cite news| url=http://www.nytimes.com/2011/11/22/sports/baseball/justin-verlander-is-first-pitcher-named-mvp-since-1992.html?src=me&amp;ref=sports | work=The New York Times | first=Tyler | last=Kepner | title=Verlander Is First Pitcher Named M.V.P. Since 1992 | date=November 21, 2011}}&lt;/ref&gt; The National League went even longer without an MVP award to a pitcher—after [[Bob Gibson]] won in 1968, no pitcher in that league was named MVP until Kershaw in 2014.&lt;ref name=Kershaw&gt;{{cite news|url=http://espn.go.com/los-angeles/mlb/story/_/id/11871556/2014-national-league-mvp-winner-clayton-kershaw-los-angeles-dodgers-named-nl-winner |title=Kershaw wins NL MVP |publisher=''ESPN.com'' |date=November 13, 2014 |accessdate=November 13, 2014}}&lt;/ref&gt;

==Chalmers Award (1911–1914)==
[[File:Ty-Cobb-1913-NPC-detail-1.jpeg|thumb|right|[[Ty Cobb]] won the first American League Chalmers Award in 1911 and was at the center of the controversy over the previous season's award.|alt=Ty Cobb looking just to the left of the camera.]]
Before the [[1910 Major League Baseball season|1910 season]], Hugh Chalmers of [[Chalmers Automobile]] announced he would present a Chalmers Model 30 automobile to the player with the highest [[batting average]] in Major League Baseball at the end of the season. The [[1910 Chalmers Award|1910 race for best average in the American League]] was between the [[Detroit Tigers]]' widely disliked&lt;ref name=&quot;ESPN&quot; /&gt;&lt;ref&gt;{{cite news|url=http://news.google.com/newspapers?id=2tkOAAAAIBAJ&amp;sjid=K2oDAAAAIBAJ&amp;pg=2104,4686963|title=Mirrors of Sport|last=Doyle|first=Havey|date=July 4, 1941|work=[[Pittsburgh Post-Gazette]]|page=18|accessdate=January 19, 2010}} {{Dead link|date=October 2010|bot=H3llBot}}&lt;/ref&gt;&lt;ref&gt;{{cite web|url=http://espn.go.com/sportscentury/features/00014142.html|title=He was a pain ... but a great pain|last=Schwartz|first=Larry|work=[[ESPN.com]]|accessdate=January 19, 2010}}&lt;/ref&gt; [[Ty Cobb]] and [[Nap Lajoie]] of the [[Cleveland Indians]]. On the last day of the season, Lajoie overtook Cobb's batting average with seven [[Bunt (baseball)|bunt]] hits against the [[Baltimore Orioles|St. Louis Browns]]. American League President [[Ban Johnson]] said a recalculation showed that Cobb had won the race anyway, and Chalmers ended up awarding cars to both players.&lt;ref name=&quot;ESPN&quot; /&gt;

The [[1911 Major League Baseball season|following season]], Chalmers created the Chalmers Award. A committee of baseball writers were to convene after the season to determine the &quot;most important and useful player to the club and to the league&quot;. Since the award was not as effective at advertising as Chalmers had hoped, it was discontinued after 1914.&lt;ref name=&quot;ESPN&quot; /&gt;

==League Awards (1922–1929)==
[[File:Babe Ruth2.jpg|thumb|right|[[Babe Ruth]] was ineligible for the award in his famous 1927 season by the rules of the American League award because he had previously won in 1923.|alt=A man in full baseball attire wears a pinstriped jersey and a hat with overlapping white &quot;N&quot; and &quot;Y&quot;. Looking to the left of the camera, he is holding a baseball upward.]]
In 1922 the American League created a new award to honor &quot;the baseball player who is of the greatest all-around service to his club&quot;.&lt;ref name=&quot;A-Rod&quot;&gt;{{cite web|url=http://www.mlb.com/players/rodriguez_alex/article.jsp?story=111405b|title=One of a kind: Another MVP for A-Rod|last=Newman|first=Mark|date=November 14, 2009|work=[[MLB.com]]|publisher=Major League Baseball|accessdate=December 5, 2009}}&lt;/ref&gt; Winners, voted on by a committee of eight baseball writers chaired by [[James Crusinberry]],&lt;ref&gt;{{cite news|url=http://news.google.com/newspapers?id=0qshAAAAIBAJ&amp;sjid=nJoFAAAAIBAJ&amp;pg=5771,1741643|title=Rogers Hornsby Voted Most Valuable Player in National League|last=Gould|first=Alan|date=December 8, 1929|work=[[Reading Eagle]]|accessdate=January 6, 2010}}&lt;/ref&gt; received a bronze medal and a cash prize.&lt;ref&gt;{{cite news|title=Player Award Goes to Hornsby again|date=December 8, 1929|work=[[The New York Times]]|page=S2}}&lt;/ref&gt; Voters were required to select one player from each team and [[player-coach]]es and prior award winners were ineligible. These flaws resulted in the award's being dropped after 1928. The National League award, without these restrictions, lasted from 1924 to 1929.&lt;ref name=&quot;ESPN&quot; /&gt;

==Baseball Writers' Association of America's Most Valuable Player (1931–present)==
The BBWAA first awarded the modern MVP after the [[1931 Major League Baseball season|1931 season]], adopting the format the National League used to distribute its league award. One writer in each city with a team filled out a ten-place ballot, with ten points for the recipient of a first-place vote, nine for a second-place vote, and so on. In 1938, the BBWAA raised the number of voters to three per city and gave 14 points for a first-place vote. The only significant change since then occurred in 1961, when the number of voters was reduced to two per league city.&lt;ref name=&quot;ESPN&quot; /&gt;
